# Garry's Launch Strategy

> "Launch something bad, quickly. Literally the rest is just re-summarized versions of that same thing." — Michael Seibel, YC

This brain distills Garry Tan's launch wisdom and Y Combinator's proven framework for launching products that people want.

## Contract

**What this brain covers:**
- Pre-launch momentum building and community cultivation
- When, where, and how to launch (Product Hunt, HN, Reddit, etc.)
- Getting your first 10-100 users who love you
- Post-launch iteration and retention strategies
- Common launch anti-patterns to avoid

**What this brain does NOT cover:**
- Fundraising timing (see separate fundraising brains)
- Enterprise sales cycles
- Regulatory-heavy launches (biotech, fintech with compliance)
- Physical product manufacturing logistics

**Prerequisites:**
- You have an MVP or "quantum of utility" to show users
- You know what problem you're solving
- You're willing to talk to strangers about your product

## The Launch Philosophy

### The Core Truth

**Launch is not a one-time event. It's a continuous process.**

Most founders treat launch like a rocket launch — one shot, everything must be perfect, timing is everything. This is wrong.

Think of launch as **always shipping**. You'll launch your MVP, then new features, then pivots, then major versions. If you spend 6 months preparing for "the big launch," you'll likely be dead before you get another chance.

> "I just talked to a team that's been preparing to launch for six months... lovingly trying to get every pixel perfect... But if you're like most startups, you will launch something, no one will care. And if it took you six months to get there, your startup is probably going to be dead before you get another chance." — Kat Mañalac, YC

### The Launch-Retention Paradox

| What Founders Think | Reality |
|---------------------|---------|
| Big launch = success | Big launch ≠ sustainable growth |
| Press coverage = users | Press coverage ≠ engaged users |
| Perfect product = happy users | Iterated product with feedback = happy users |
| Launch once, scale forever | Launch repeatedly, iterate always |

## Phase 1: Pre-Launch (The Quiet Phase)

### The Silent Launch (Do This First)

Before you have a fully functioning product, launch a landing page. You need:

1. **Domain name** — buy it today
2. **Company name** — clear and memorable  
3. **One-line pitch** — what you do in 10 words
4. **Short description** — 2-3 sentences max
5. **Contact/CTA** — "Get in touch" or "Join waitlist"
6. **Email capture** — start building your list NOW

**Example (Zn - random Startup School company):**
```
Zn
AI-powered design tools for non-designers

Zn helps startup founders create beautiful landing pages, 
app icons, and marketing assets without hiring a designer.

Get in touch →
```

**Platforms for silent launch:**
- Carrd, Webflow, or simple HTML — 1 day max
- Product Hunt Ship — for waitlist building
- Substack — for founder narrative + email capture

### Build Your Pre-Launch List

Gadi Evron (Cymmetria) had a massive TechCrunch launch. His secret? **Years of building an email list.**

Every person he met, every conversation about his startup — he added them to an email list. By launch day, he had hundreds of people ready to amplify his message.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Create a simple "updates list" signup
- [ ] Add every person you discuss your startup with
- [ ] Send semi-regular updates (bi-weekly minimum)
- [ ] Ask for help spreading the word on launch day

> "You would be really surprised by who comes out of the woodwork to help when you ask for it."

### The Friends & Family Launch

**Purpose:** Practice your pitch, get initial feedback, confirm the problem exists.

**Process:**
1. Share with 5-10 people you trust
2. Sit next to them while they use it
3. Don't explain — watch them struggle
4. Take notes on confusion points
5. **Get out of this phase FAST**

**Warning:** Your friends and family are probably NOT your ideal users. Reddit's founders launched to their YC batch (8 companies). If they'd launched to their parents, they would have heard "What the hell is this?"

### Validate with Strangers (The IKEA Test)

Lugg (on-demand movers) didn't have an app when they validated their idea. They:

1. Rented a truck
2. Sat outside IKEA
3. Watched people struggling to tie mattresses to cars
4. Ran up and said: "Wouldn't it be cool if you could push a button and someone with a truck would help?"
5. When people said yes, they drove their truck around and helped

**The lesson:** You don't need code to validate. You need hustle.

## Phase 2: Launch Day (The Many Launches)

### Types of Launches (Do Them All)

Launch is not one event. It's a sequence:

| Launch Type | When | Goal |
|-------------|------|------|
| Silent Launch | Idea stage | Email capture, practice pitch |
| Friends & Family | MVP ready | Initial feedback, bug finding |
| Stranger Launch | Pre-product | Problem validation |
| Online Communities | MVP working | First real users |
| Product Hunt | Ready for scale | Broad awareness |
| Hacker News | Technical product | Technical community |
| Press/Bloggers | Traction proven | Wider audience |
| New Features | Always | Continuous engagement |

### The Community Launch (Your First Real Users)

**Where to launch:**
- **Hacker News** — Show HN, technical audiences
- **Product Hunt** — Early adopter community
- **Reddit** — Find your specific subreddit
- **Startup School Forum** — 40,000 founders
- **Industry-specific communities** — Designer News, Indie Hackers, etc.

**Magic's Story:**
Magic (YC W15) was building a blood pressure app. They tested an idea: "Text this number and we'll make anything happen like magic." A friend shared it on Reddit and Hacker News. **40,000 people signed up overnight.**

This is an extreme case. But it proves: **put yourself out there.**

### How to Write a Launch Post

**The Golden Rule:** Write like you talk. Do NOT talk like a marketing robot.

**Structure:**
```
Hi [Community],

I'm [Name], and I built [Product].

[One sentence: What it does]
[One sentence: Why you built it / the problem]
[One paragraph: Interesting insight from talking to users]

I'd love your feedback. Happy to answer questions about:
- [Expertise area 1]
- [Expertise area 2]
- [Expertise area 3]

[Link]
```

**Do:**
- Share surprising things you learned from users
- Be intellectually curious
- Offer to answer specific questions
- Show vulnerability ("We know X is broken")

**Don't:**
- Use jargon or marketing speak
- Claim to "revolutionize" or "disrupt"
- Use buzzwords ("AI-powered" is fine if true, not if it's just an if-statement)
- Sound like a press release

### Platform-Specific Tactics

**Product Hunt:**
- Launch Tuesday-Thursday, early AM PST
- Prepare assets: hero image, GIF demo, maker comment
- Have your team upvote in the first hour
- Respond to EVERY comment within the first 4 hours
- Hunter matters less than product quality

**Hacker News (Show HN):**
- Technical depth wins
- "Ask HN" posts often do better than "Show HN"
- Be ready for tough feedback
- The community loves learning — share something surprising
- Not just for dev tools! 70 Million Jobs (jobs for formerly incarcerated) was a top launch

**Reddit:**
- Find the SPECIFIC subreddit (not just r/startups)
- Read the rules — every subreddit is different
- Ask for advice, not upvotes
- Post at optimal times for that subreddit
- Engage in comments genuinely

### The Waitlist Launch (Scarcity Marketing)

When Magic got 40,000 signups, they couldn't serve everyone. So they:

1. Created a waitlist
2. Let people skip the line by tweeting about Magic
3. Built viral elements into the product

**Superhuman** did this perfectly:
- Request access to join
- Current users can refer you to skip the line
- "Sent via Superhuman" in email signatures drives referrals

**When to use:**
- Limited capacity (naturally constrained)
- High-touch onboarding required
- Product not fully ready for scale
- Want to create FOMO

## Phase 3: Post-Launch (The Real Work)

### The Retention Check

After launch, ask one question:

> "Did users come back the next day/week?"

If the problem is real and your solution works, users will return. If they don't, you have a retention problem, not a launch problem.

**Michael Seibel's retention test:**
"If it's a problem they have every day, and you introduced a user to it, did they come back the next day? I've never seen a product that solves a problem people have every day, that actually solves the problem, where a user just stops using it because, like, whatever."

### Do Things That Don't Scale

This is Paul Graham's most famous advice, and it's critical post-launch.

**Examples:**
- **Airbnb:** Personally photographed every host's apartment
- **Stripe:** Collison brothers installed integrations themselves ("Collison installation")
- **Wufoo:** Hand-written thank you notes to every new user
- **Lugg:** Founders drove the trucks themselves

**Why it works:**
1. You learn what users actually want
2. You build relationships with early adopters
3. You find bugs before users do
4. The unscalable things become your company DNA

> "Garry Tan pointed out an interesting trap founders fall into in the beginning. They want so much to seem big that they imitate even the flaws of big companies, like indifference to individual users. This seems to them more 'professional.' Actually it's better to embrace the fact that you're small and use whatever advantages that brings." — Paul Graham

### The 90/10 Solution

Paul Buchheit's advice: Find a way to accomplish **90% of what you want with 10% of the work.**

Post-launch, you'll get a flood of feature requests. Don't build them all. Look for:
- The quick fix that solves most of the problem
- The workaround that buys you time
- The existing tool/integration that removes the need

A 90% solution available now beats a 100% solution that takes months.

### Iterate vs. Pivot

**Iterate:** Keep the problem, keep the customer, fix the solution.

**Pivot:** Change the problem, change the customer, or change fundamentally.

Michael Seibel's analogy:
> "You have a screwdriver that's not screwing anything in. Don't wonder what other problems it could solve (cooking? cleaning?). Keep the mechanic, keep the problem (screwing things in), fix the fucking screwdriver."

**Hold tightly:**
- The problem you're solving
- The customer you're serving

**Hold loosely:**
- The specific solution/feature set

### Growth vs. Retention (Do Both)

Founders always ask: "Should I focus on growth or retention?"

Answer: Both. It's not a choice.

> "What's more important, taking a shower or going to the bathroom? I'd like you to do both." — Michael Seibel

**Pre-product-market fit:**
- Retention matters more
- Don't scale prematurely
- Do things that don't scale

**Post-product-market fit:**
- Growth accelerates
- Start building scalable systems
- But don't lose the personal touch

## Decision Frameworks

### The Launch Readiness Checklist

**Launch NOW if:**
- [ ] Your product has a "quantum of utility" — it does ONE thing that helps someone
- [ ] You can get your first 10 users manually
- [ ] You're embarrassed by your product (good sign — means you shipped fast)
- [ ] You've been working on it for more than 1 month without user feedback

**Wait to launch if:**
- [ ] The product literally doesn't work (crashes, data loss)
- [ ] You have no way to contact/interact with users
- [ ] You're in a regulated industry requiring approval
- [ ] You're building hard tech (rockets, biotech) where MVP = simple website explaining the vision

### Where Should You Launch?

**For B2B/Dev Tools:**
1. Hacker News (Show HN)
2. Product Hunt
3. Industry-specific communities
4. Direct outreach (LinkedIn, email)

**For Consumer:**
1. Reddit (find your subreddit)
2. Product Hunt
3. TikTok/Instagram (visual products)
4. Twitter/X (build in public)

**For Vertical/Niche:**
1. The ONE community where your users hang out
2. Industry forums
3. Trade associations
4. Direct sales (B2B)

### The Big Launch Fallacy

**Don't do:**
- Launch parties (waste of money)
- Embargoed press releases (waste of time)
- Coordinating multiple outlets simultaneously (pointless)
- Waiting for "perfect" timing (it doesn't exist)

**Do instead:**
- Launch to one community at a time
- Learn from each launch
- Iterate between launches
- Build your own email list

> "How many here remember the day Google launched? Facebook? Twitter? It turns out that launches aren't that special at all." — Michael Seibel

## Anti-Patterns (What Not to Do)

### 1. The Perfectionist Trap

**Symptoms:**
- Spending months on pixel-perfect design
- Adding "just one more feature" before launch
- Constantly changing the MVP without shipping

**The Fix:**
Launch something embarrassingly simple. Your first grade paper wasn't perfect — your MVP won't be either.

### 2. The Big Bang Launch

**Symptoms:**
- Planning a "launch day" with press, events, announcements
- Spending money on launch parties
- Trying to coordinate coverage across multiple outlets

**The Fix:**
Launch quietly to friends, then strangers, then communities. Let the press come to you when you have traction.

### 3. The Build-It-And-They-Will-Come Fallacy

**Symptoms:**
- Believing great products market themselves
- Waiting for viral growth
- Refusing to do manual user acquisition

**The Fix:**
Startups take off because founders MAKE them take off. Recruit users manually. Every. Single. One.

### 4. The Feature Creep Death Spiral

**Symptoms:**
- Adding features for every user request
- Trying to solve every problem for every user
- MVP that does 10 things poorly instead of 1 thing well

**The Fix:**
Never ask users for features. Ask them for problems. Then YOU decide the solution.

### 5. The Big Customer Trap

**Symptoms:**
- Chasing deals with large companies
- Customizing your product for one big potential customer
- Spending months on negotiations

**The Fix:**
Small deals with small customers > big deals that never close. Start small.

### 6. The Scale-Too-Early Trap

**Symptoms:**
- Building automation before you have product-market fit
- Hiring a sales team before founders have sold
- Optimizing for 1000 users when you have 10

**The Fix:**
Do things that don't scale until you have clear product-market fit. Then automate.

### 7. The Competitor Obsession

**Symptoms:**
- Watching competitor moves daily
- Pivoting because a competitor launched something
- Copying competitor features

**The Fix:**
Startups die of suicide, not murder. Ignore competitors. Focus on your users.

## The GBrain Launch Case Study

Garry Tan launched GBrain (an AI product) to 17,888 pages of usage in 12 days. How?

### What We Know:
1. **Shipped fast** — No 6-month planning cycle
2. **Had existing audience** — 500K+ Twitter followers
3. **Built in public** — Shared progress openly
4. **Solved his own problem** — Dogfooded the product
5. **Leveraged existing platforms** — AI hype cycle + distribution

### Lessons:
- Your audience compounds — start building it now
- Timing matters (AI wave in 2024-2025)
- Launch velocity matters more than launch perfection
- Personal brand + product = distribution

## The Pocket Guide (TL;DR)

### Pre-Launch
- [ ] Silent launch with email capture
- [ ] Build pre-launch list (friends, potential users)
- [ ] Friends & family launch for feedback
- [ ] Validate with strangers (hustle, not code)

### Launch
- [ ] Launch to online communities (HN, PH, Reddit)
- [ ] Write like you talk (no marketing speak)
- [ ] Respond to every comment
- [ ] Do waitlist/viral mechanics if capacity-limited

### Post-Launch
- [ ] Do things that don't scale
- [ ] Check: Did users come back?
- [ ] Find 90/10 solutions
- [ ] Iterate on solution, not problem
- [ ] Balance growth AND retention

### Remember
- **Launch is continuous, not one-time**
- **Ship something bad, quickly**
- **10 customers who love you > 1000 who kinda like you**
- **Write code, talk to users**
- **Big launches don't matter — happy users do**
